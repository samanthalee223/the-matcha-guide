What is Matcha?

Matcha is a fine milled powder made from the shade-grown plant Camellia Sinensis which also produces oolong and other green teas. Matcha originates from China but has become an important part of Japanese tea culture.

How is Matcha Made?

Step 1

The tea leaves are grown in the shade which produces more chlorophyll and produces more antioxidants and amino acids. This process is done two to six weeks before harvesting.

Step 2

The youngest leaves are hand picked for the first harvest in mid-April to mid-May and are the highest quality. The second harvest occurs around 40 days after.

Step 3

The harvested leaves are steamed to prevent them from oxidizing and dried. The dried tea leaves are crushed into tencha, the result before the final matcha powder is made.

Step 4

The crushed leaves are de-stemmed and graded before being stored in a refrigerated room.

Step 5

Tea processors grind the tencha into a fine powder, creating matcha.

What Does Matcha Taste Like?

Depending on the blend of matcha and the way it is prepared, matcha can taste vastly different and bring out different notes. Identifying these flavours takes experience, so if you are a beginner, this can be very intimidating and can make it harder to know what your preferences are.

There is usually a description of the flavours using these terms on the matcha packaging. A good blend of matcha will have the perfect balance of all tasting notes. What is most important for beginners is finding a blend that suits your preferences and budget and easing into the flavours of matcha.

Here is a breakdown of common ways to describe the flavours of matcha in English:

Vegetal:

Vegetal refers to the slightly grassy, vegetable-like flavours in matcha. Since matcha is made from dried leaves, its vegetal notes are stronger than regular green tea.

Sweetness:

Plain matcha with no milk or sweeteners has a subtle sweetness that balances out the stronger flavours in matcha. This usually appears as a sweet aftertaste after an initial bitter taste.

Umami:

Umami is probably the hardest to describe and identify. In other dishes, umami is a Japanese expression used to describe foods like miso soup that are savoury, rich, and slightly oceanic. Umami in matcha is a result of the shading process while growing the leaves and the increased production of amino acids like L-theanine.

Astringency:

In English, bitterness has a negative connotation, but in Japanese there is a distinction between “good” bitterness and “bad” bitterness. The closest term to describe matcha’s earthy bitterness is astringency. Low quality matcha may taste unpleasantly bitter, but high quality matcha will usually have a slight astringency on the first sip.

Traditional Japanese Tea Ceremony

Usucha (Thin Tea)

Usucha is the most common way to drink matcha and is a lighter concentration compared to Koicha. Usucha has a thin consistency and is made in small portions for individuals to drink.

Koicha (Thick Tea)

Koicha is prepared with a higher concentration of matcha and shared between guests from a single bowl. It has a thick paste consistency made using the highest quality leaves, making it the best way to taste a matcha powder's true flavours.

Caffeine: Matcha vs. Coffee

Like coffee, matcha contains caffeine. However, the L-theanine provides a more balanced, milder energy boost for a longer period of time compared to the shorter, larger boost from coffee.

Health Benefits

Due to shade growing techniques, matcha is rich in antioxidants and L-theanine, an amino acid that helps with relaxation while giving you a boost of energy. L-theanine is known to reduce stress, improve sleep quality, and lessen the negative effects of caffeine. Since the leaves are ground into a fine powder, matcha contains nutrients from the whole plant.
